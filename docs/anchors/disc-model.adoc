====
Full Name:: DISC Model of Behavioural Styles

Also known as:: DISC, DiSC, Marston's DISC

[discrete]
== *Core Concepts*:

Four Behavioural Styles:: The modern model sorts behaviour into four quadrants:
* *Dominance (D)* — direct, results-driven, assertive; focus on the *what*
* *Influence (I)* — outgoing, enthusiastic, persuasive; focus on *people*
* *Steadiness (S)* — patient, dependable, cooperative; focus on *pace* and stability
* *Conscientiousness (C)* — precise, analytical, quality-focused; focus on *how*

Marston's Original Terms:: In _Emotions of Normal People_ (1928) Marston named the four as Dominance, Inducement, Submission, and Compliance; the modern instruments relabel them as above.

Two Axes:: The quadrants arise from two dimensions — how a person perceives the environment (favourable vs. antagonistic) and their sense of control within it (more vs. less powerful).

Style, Not Ability:: DISC describes a behavioural *style* and its communication preferences, not competence or fixed character.

Theory vs. Instrument:: Marston proposed the *theory* but never built an assessment or patented it. The self-assessment tools came later (Walter Clarke's Activity Vector Analysis, 1956; John Geier's DiSC, 1970s) and are today commercial products from many vendors.

Key Proponents:: William Moulton Marston (theory, 1928); assessment instruments by Walter Clarke and later John Geier and commercial publishers.

[discrete]
== *When to Use*:

For reliable activation, name the model *and the task* — "map this person's communication style onto the DISC quadrants" — rather than the bare term.

* Framing communication and collaboration preferences within a team (D/I/S/C tendencies as a shared shorthand)
* Facilitation, coaching, or workshop settings where a lightweight behavioural vocabulary helps dialogue
* Tailoring how a message is delivered to a described counterpart's style
* Onboarding conversations about working styles

[discrete]
== *Common Misunderstandings*:

* ❌ "My DISC type says what I can do" — it describes a behavioural preference, not ability
* ❌ "DISC is scientifically validated" — it is not; it predicts job performance no better than chance
* ✓ "DISC is a conversational starting point" — correct use is exploratory dialogue, never selection or diagnosis
====

== *Criticism*:

* Via the https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DISC_assessment[DISC assessment overview] — classified as *pseudoscientific*: the scientific validity of DISC has not been demonstrated, and the assessments have shown *no ability to predict job performance* (a 2013 German study found reliability "largely" met but validity "not at all"). DISC data are better explained as combinations of the Big Five than as independent traits.
* Its apparent accuracy is partly the *Barnum effect* — people accept vague, broadly flattering descriptions as personally true. Marston's four categories ignore personality science after 1920.

[discrete]
== *Current Status*:

* Commercially popular in corporate training and team-building yet scientifically discredited; because Marston never patented the theory, many vendors sell competing DISC instruments. Treat it as a starting point for reflection and dialogue, never as a diagnostic or hiring tool; this is why the anchor carries an `:advisory:` flag. The empirically validated alternatives are the Big Five / OCEAN and HEXACO.
